## 1. ACADEMIC LITERATURE ON FOOTBALL TACTICS

### 1.1 Tactical Periodization (Periodização Tática)

Tactical Periodization (TP) was conceived by Professor **Vítor Frade** at the Faculty of Sport of the University of Porto (FADEUP) in the 1970s, formally articulated through his PhD work and disseminated through coaches such as Jesualdo Ferreira, Carlos Queiroz, José Mourinho, André Villas-Boas and Rui Faria. The methodology rejects the classical separation of "physical / technical / tactical / psychological" preparation and instead organises every training task around the **Game Model** (*Modelo de Jogo*), expressed as **Principles, Sub-Principles, and Sub-Sub-Principles** distributed across the **Four Moments of the Game**: offensive organisation, defensive organisation, attacking transition and defensive transition.

The most influential secondary literature includes **Tamarit (2007, 2013)** — *¿Qué es la Periodización Táctica?* and *Periodización Táctica vs Periodización Táctica*; **Oliveira, Amieiro, Resende & Barreto (2006)** — *Mourinho: Por Qué Tantas Victorias?*; and the academic synthesis by **Delgado-Bordonau & Mendez-Villanueva (2012)** in the *NSCA Coach*, which framed TP for an Anglophone strength-and-conditioning readership. A formal **systematic review by Afonso, Nikolaidis, Sousa & Mesquita (2017, *Frontiers in Physiology*)** — "A Systematic Review of Research on Tactical Periodization: Absence of Empirical Data, Burden of Proof, and Benefit of Doubt" — concluded that TP, despite massive applied uptake, suffers from a notable scarcity of randomised empirical studies, with most outputs being descriptive case studies or master's dissertations from FADEUP (Martins 2003; Resende 2002; Carvalho).

The three **methodological principles** are:

- **Specificity** (the *supraprinciple*): every task must contain the cognitive and behavioural logic of the Game Model.
- **Horizontal Alternation in Specificity**: across the **Morphocycle** (or *padrão semanal*), the dominant neuromuscular / emotional contraction is alternated day-to-day so that no two consecutive sessions stress the same sub-dynamic — Tension (eccentric, short, intense, small spaces) → Duration (medium-medium, multi-sectoral, aerobic-tactical) → Speed/Velocity (short actions, full recovery, decision-making under speed).
- **Complex Progression**: hierarchy of principles is progressed not linearly but in a fractal, spiral manner across morphocycles.

### 1.2 Ecological Dynamics and the Constraints-Led Approach (CLA)

The ecological-dynamical school, anchored by **Keith Davids (Sheffield Hallam)**, **Duarte Araújo (Universidade de Lisboa)**, **Ian Renshaw**, **Jia Yi Chow**, **Carl Woods** and **Bruno Travassos**, frames players and teams as **complex adaptive systems** in which behaviour emerges from the interaction of **task, environmental and individual constraints** (Newell, 1986). Core canonical works include Araújo, Davids & Hristovski (2006) *"The ecological dynamics of decision making in sport"* in *Psychology of Sport and Exercise*; Pinder, Davids, Renshaw & Araújo (2011) *"Representative learning design"* in *Journal of Sport & Exercise Psychology*; Renshaw, Chow, Davids & Hammond (2010); and the recent **Chow, Davids & Araújo (2025), *Ecological Dynamics Approach to Football*** (Routledge). The framework rejects information-processing models (decision → planning → execution) in favour of **direct perception of affordances** (Gibson, 1979) and **perception-action coupling**.

### 1.3 Tactical Periodization vs Differential Learning vs Game-Based Approaches

**Differential Learning (DL)**, developed by **Wolfgang Schöllhorn** at Mainz, maximises movement variability and *stochastic perturbation* on the premise that the system self-organises to a more robust attractor (Schöllhorn et al., 2006, *Journal of Sports Sciences*; 2012). **Game-Based Approaches (GBA)** — encompassing Teaching Games for Understanding (Bunker & Thorpe, 1982), Game Sense (Light, 2004) and Tactical Games Approach (Mitchell, Oslin & Griffin) — share a constructivist epistemology. Practically: TP is *deductive* (top-down from a Game Model); CLA/DL are *inductive* (bottom-up emergence). Both converge on the rejection of decontextualised drills.

### 1.4 Skill Acquisition Science

The Liverpool John Moores group — **A. Mark Williams, Paul Ford, André Roca, Joe Causer** — has produced the dominant empirical corpus on football expertise: Ford, Yates & Williams (2010, *J Sports Sci*) on the activities of elite English youth coaches; Roca, Ford, McRobert & Williams (2011, 2013) on perceptual-cognitive expertise in defenders; Williams & Ford's chapter in Williams (2013) *Science and Soccer III* (Routledge).

### 1.5 Notational Analysis Evolution

The lineage from **Charles Reep** (1950s, manual coding) through **Mike Hughes & Ian Franks** — *Notational Analysis of Sport* (1997, 2nd ed. 2004) — to contemporary computer vision (Hawk-Eye, ChyronHego, Second Spectrum) is documented in **Sarmento, Marcelino, Anguera, Campaniço, Matos & Leitão (2014) "Match analysis in football: a systematic review", *Journal of Sports Sciences* 32(20): 1831–1843**, which screened over 2,800 records and synthesised 77 studies. Subsequent Sarmento reviews include *"What performance analysts need to know about research trends in association football (2012–2016)"* (Sports Medicine, 2018) and the pressing-focused review of Forcher et al. (2022).

### 1.6 Collective Tactical Behaviour: Emergence and Synergies

Self-organisation in football is examined by Duarte, Araújo, Correia & Davids (2012) "Sports teams as superorganisms" (*Sports Medicine* 42(8): 633–642), and Travassos, Davids, Araújo & Esteves (2013) "Performance analysis in team sports" (*Int J Performance Analysis in Sport*).

## 2. POSITIONS — PHYSIOLOGICAL DEEP DIVE

The reference matrix below synthesises **Bradley et al. (2009) *J Sports Sci* 27(2): 159–168**; **Di Salvo et al. (2007) *Int J Sports Med* 28: 222–227** and Di Salvo et al. (2009, 2010); **Mohr, Krustrup & Bangsbo (2003) *J Sports Sci* 21(7): 519–528**; **Dellal et al. (2011) *J Strength Cond Res*** comparing La Liga and Premier League by position; **Bush, Barnes, Archer, Hogg & Bradley (2015) *Human Movement Science*** on EPL position-specific evolution 2006–2013; and **Modric, Versic, Sekulic & Liposek (2019)** in *Sports* on positional differences in the Croatian first division.

### 2.1 Goalkeepers (GK)
- **Total distance**: 4,000–6,000 m/match.
- HIR >19.8 km/h: typically <100 m. Sprints >25.2 km/h: ~5–10.
- Cognitive: highest *visual search field*, anticipation studies by Savelsbergh, Williams, Van der Kamp & Ward (2002, *J Sports Sci*) on penalty saving — elite keepers fixate later but on more relevant cues. Otte, Davids, Millar & Klatt (2019, *Frontiers in Psychology*) reframed the goalkeeper through ecological dynamics as a "hybrid role" — sweeper-keeper affordances.
- Anthropometry: tallest position (mean ~1.88–1.92 m), high reactive strength (RSI), strong eccentric hamstring (decisive in diving).

### 2.2 Centre-Backs (CB)
- Total distance: **9,800–10,500 m** (Bradley 2009 reported CBs as covering the least distance).
- HIR >19.8 km/h: **~1,834 m (SD 256)** (Bradley 2009 — significantly less than every other outfield position, p<0.01).
- Sprints >25.2 km/h: 8–12; peak speeds 28–31 km/h.
- Cognitive profile (Jordet et al., 2020 *Frontiers in Psychology*; McGuckian et al., 2021 *J Sports Sci*): **central defenders show the highest scanning frequency** alongside central midfielders, consistent with high information demands and back-to-play orientation.
- Anthropometry: 1.85–1.92 m; aerial duels >70% win rate at elite level.

### 2.3 Full-Backs / Wing-Backs (FB)
- Total distance: **10,500–11,500 m**.
- HIR: **~2,605 m (SD 387)** (Bradley 2009).
- Bush et al. (2015) showed full-backs displayed the **largest evolutionary increase in HIR (+35%, ES 1.3)** between 2006–07 and 2012–13, reflecting the tactical migration toward inverted/overlapping full-backs.
- Sprints: 20–30/match; peak speed often the highest non-attacking values (~32 km/h).
- VO2max: 60–65 ml·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹; high repeated-sprint ability (RSA).

### 2.4 Defensive Midfielders (DM / Volante / Pivot)
- Total distance: **11,000–12,000 m**.
- HIR: 2,300–2,700 m; sprints fewer than wide players but more accelerations/decelerations per minute.
- Highest **passes per match** and **interception/recovery frequency**.
- Executive function profile (Vestberg, Gustafson, Maurex, Ingvar & Petrovic, 2012, *PLOS ONE*): central midfielders disproportionately drive the **design fluency** and **working-memory updating** advantage of elite over sub-elite players.

### 2.5 Central Midfielders (CM)
- **Largest total distance: 11,500–12,300 m** (Mohr et al. 2003; Di Salvo 2007).
- HIR: **2,825 m (SD 473)** (Bradley 2009).
- VO2max: **highest among outfielders — 60–67 ml·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹** (Stølen, Chamari, Castagna & Wisløff, 2005, *Sports Medicine* 35(6): 501–536).
- Maximal aerobic speed (MAS): 16.5–17.5 km/h.
- Scanning frequency: highest pre-reception scans/s (Jordet 2013, 2020).

### 2.6 Attacking Midfielders (AM / Number 10)
- Total distance: 10,800–11,500 m.
- HIR: 2,400–2,800 m; high deceleration density.
- Decision-making under pressure (Roca, Ford, McRobert & Williams, 2011 *Cognitive Processing*; 2013 *J Experimental Psychology: Applied*): AMs show superior option generation rates (Take-The-First heuristic literature, Johnson & Raab, 2003).

### 2.7 Wingers / Wide Midfielders (WM)
- Total distance: 10,800–11,500 m.
- **Highest HIR: 3,138 m (SD 565)** (Bradley 2009, significantly greater than CM p=0.04; FB and CF p<0.01).
- Highest sprint count (28–35) and accelerations/match (>50 above 3 m·s⁻²).
- Bradley et al. (2009) also documented ~**20% decline in HIR in the last 15 min** in wide midfielders (467 m vs 605 m in the first 15 min), the classic *positional fatigue signature*.

### 2.8 Centre-Forwards (CF)
- Total distance: **10,200–10,800 m**.
- HIR: **2,341 m (SD 575)**; among the highest peak sprint velocities (~30.4 ± 1.6 km/h, Hungarian/EPL comparisons).
- Anaerobic glycolytic capacity decisive; high reactive strength (jump and ground-contact economy).
- Vertical jump (CMJ): 38–48 cm at elite level (Loturco et al., 2015).

## 3. PRESSING SYSTEMS — ACADEMIC RESEARCH

### 3.1 Definitions and Classification

Pressing is operationalised as the **collective application of spatial-temporal pressure on the ball carrier and likely receivers with the intent of inducing turnover** (Merckx et al., 2021; Low, Boas, Meyer, Lames et al., 2021 *Science and Medicine in Football*). Sarmento's reviews and Forcher, Forcher, Jekauc, Wäsche, Woll, Gröben & Altmann (2022) "Tactical behavior in association football: a systematic review" (*Frontiers in Psychology*) classify pressing by **block height** (high, mid, low), **trigger** (pass to FB, back-pass to CB, heavy touch, square pass), and **synchronisation pattern** (zonal, man-oriented, hybrid).

### 3.2 The Quantitative Metrics: PPDA, Pressure, exPress

- **PPDA (Passes per Defensive Action)** was popularised by **Colin Trainor (2014, StatsBomb)** and adopted academically by **Fernandez-Navarro, Fradua, Zubillaga, Ford & McRobert (2016) "Attacking and defensive styles of play in soccer: analysis of Spanish and English elite teams", *Journal of Sports Sciences* 34(24): 2195–2204**, where they extracted six attacking and two defensive playing styles via principal component analysis and showed PPDA discriminates between *Pressing Possession* and *Direct Counter-Pressing* styles.
- **Pressing Intensity / Pressure on the ball carrier** based on distance-to-opponent and closing velocity was formalised by **Andrienko et al. (2017) "Visual analysis of pressure in football", *Data Mining and Knowledge Discovery* 31(6): 1793–1839**.
- **Bauer & Anzer (2021) "Data-driven detection of counterpressing in professional football", *Data Mining and Knowledge Discovery* 35: 2009–2049** built an XGBoost classifier on Bundesliga synchronised positional + event data, defining counterpressing as the team-tactical attempt to recover the ball within the first ~6 s after possession loss. They demonstrated that counterpressing's risk-reward profile is strongly team-dependent.
- **Anzer, Bauer & Brefeld (2021) "The origins of goals in the German Bundesliga", *J Sports Sci* 39(22): 2525–2544** clustered goal-creating sequences and found that high-pressing recoveries in the attacking third yield disproportionate xG.
- **exPress (Gu, Na, Pei & De Silva, 2024 MIT Sloan)** and the **VPEP** model (Robberechts) extend valuation to *contextual* pressing efficiency.

### 3.3 Counterpressing (Gegenpressing)

**Vogelbein, Nopp & Hökelmann (2014) "Defensive transition in soccer", *Journal of Sports Sciences* 32(11): 1076–1083** showed in 306 Bundesliga matches that **reaction time to defensive transition** (time-to-pressure after possession loss) is significantly faster in winning teams. Goes, Meerhoff, Bueno, Rodrigues, Moura, Brink, Elferink-Gemser, Knobbe, Cunha & Lemmink (2021) *European Journal of Sport Science* on the value of pass-line breaking complements this.

### 3.4 Pressing Fatigue and the Second-Half Decrement

Bradley et al. (2009, *J Sports Sci*) and Mohr et al. (2003, *J Sports Sci*) established the **~20% HIR drop in the final 15 minutes**, with **transient fatigue** following the most intense 5-min period (Mohr — the next 5-min period shows ~12% reduction). Carling et al. (*Sports Medicine* 2008; Carling, Bradley, McCall & Dupont, 2016 *J Sports Sci*) extended this to recovery kinetics across consecutive fixtures.

### 3.5 Pressing across Formations

Bradley, Carling, Archer, Roberts, Dodds, Di Mascio, Paul, Diaz, Peart & Krustrup (2011) "The effect of playing formation on high-intensity running and technical profiles in English FA Premier League soccer matches", *J Sports Sci* 29(8): 821–830 — analysed 4-4-2 vs 4-3-3 vs 4-5-1 on 153 EPL players. Overall HIR did not differ significantly across formations, but in possession, 4-5-1 reduced very-high-intensity running.

## 4. TRAINING FOCUS — ACADEMIC FOUNDATIONS

### 4.1 The Pattern Morphocycle (Frade)

Assuming a Sunday-to-Sunday week (single match): **MD+1** active recovery / regeneration (or day off); **MD+2** (Tuesday) recovery training, low neural and metabolic load; **MD–4** (Wednesday) "Force/Strength" sub-dynamic — short, intense, fragmented spaces (3v3, 4v4 in 25–30 m), eccentric-dominant ranges, sub-principles trained at the *intra-sectoral* level; **MD–3** (Thursday) "Endurance/Duration" sub-dynamic — medium duration, medium-large spaces, multi-sector and inter-sectoral game scenarios (8v8, 9v9 + GK on 60×45 m), the most fatiguing day; **MD–2** (Friday) "Speed/Velocity" sub-dynamic — short actions with full recovery, decision-making at speed, set-piece work; **MD–1** (Saturday) pre-activation / strategy walkthrough; **MD** Match Day.

### 4.2 Small-Sided Games — Systematic Review Evidence

- **Hill-Haas, Dawson, Impellizzeri & Coutts (2011) "Physiology of small-sided games training in football", *Sports Medicine* 41(3): 199–220** is the foundational review.
- **Bujalance-Moreno, Latorre-Román & García-Pinillos (2019) *Journal of Sports Sciences*** is the dominant systematic review.
- **Clemente, Praça, Aquino, Castillo, Raya-González, Rico-González, Afonso, Sarmento, Silva, Silva & Ramirez-Campillo (2023) "Effects of pitch size on soccer players' physiological, physical, technical, and tactical responses during small-sided games: a meta-analytical comparison", *Biology of Sport* 40(1): 111–147** — 41 studies meta-analysed. Compared to smaller pitches, **larger pitches induced**: HR (p<0.001, ES=0.50), RPE (ES=0.70), total distance (ES=1.95), HSR (ES=1.20), stretch index (ES=1.02), surface area (ES=1.54). Technical responses did not differ meaningfully.
- **Aguiar, Botelho, Lago, Maças & Sampaio (2012)** in *J Human Kinetics* on tactical responses; **Owen, Wong, Paul & Dellal (2014)** in *J Strength Cond Res* on positional SSGs.

### 4.3 HIIT in Football — The Buchheit-Laursen Framework

**Buchheit & Laursen (2013) "High-intensity interval training, solutions to the programming puzzle, Parts I & II", *Sports Medicine* 43(5): 313–338; 43(10): 927–954** classified HIIT into:

1. **Short intervals** (10–30 s work : 10–30 s rest, e.g. 15"/15" at 100–110% MAS) — high VO2 time, low neuromuscular load.
2. **Long intervals** (2–5 min at 90–95% HRmax) — VO2max and cardiac stroke volume.
3. **Repeated-sprint training (RSS)** — 5–10 × 4–6 s all-out, 20 s recovery.
4. **Sprint interval training (SIT)** — 4–6 × 30 s Wingate-style.
5. **Game-Based HIIT (GBHIIT / SSGs as HIIT)**.

**Wahl, Güldner & Mester (2014) "Effects and sustainability of a 13-day high-intensity shock microcycle in soccer", *J Sports Sci Med* 13: 259–265** — demonstrated **+5–8% VO2max** and Yo-Yo IR2 gains sustained for 4 weeks. Dolci et al. (2020) confirmed shock microcycles are efficacious in compressed preparatory blocks.

### 4.4 Strength, Power, Plyometrics

- **Suchomel, Nimphius & Stone (2016) "The Importance of Muscular Strength in Athletic Performance", *Sports Medicine* 46(10): 1419–1449**.
- **Sander, Keiner, Wirth & Schmidtbleicher (2013)** on long-term strength development in elite youth.
- **Loturco et al. (2015, 2018, 2019)** in *J Strength Cond Res* and *Int J Sports Physiol Perform* — repeatedly shown that **half-squat 1RM and mean propulsive power at optimum loads correlate r = 0.6–0.8 with 10/20-m sprint and CMJ** in Brazilian Série A players.
- **Slimani, Chamari, Miarka, Del Vecchio & Chéour (2016)** meta-analysis on plyometric training in soccer — pooled effect on jump performance ES ≈ 0.7.

### 4.5 Recovery Science

**Nédélec, McCall, Carling, Legall, Berthoin & Dupont (2012, 2013) "Recovery in soccer", *Sports Medicine* — two-part review**. Halson (2014) "Monitoring training load to understand fatigue in athletes" *Sports Medicine* 44(Suppl 2): 139–147. Hausswirth & Mujika eds., *Recovery for Performance in Sport* (Human Kinetics, 2013). Cold-water immersion ES ~0.3 on next-day CMJ; sleep extension (Mah et al., 2011, Stanford) improves sprint and reaction.

### 4.6 Perceptual-Cognitive Training

Williams, Ford, Eccles & Ward (2011) "Perceptual-cognitive expertise in sport and its acquisition" *Applied Cognitive Psychology*; Roca & Williams (2017) on video-based decision training (ES 0.4–0.9 across studies). **Vickers' Quiet Eye** (1996, 2007) — longer fixation duration on relevant cue prior to motor execution; in football applied to penalties (Wood & Wilson, 2011 *Cognitive Processing*).

### 4.7 Training Load Monitoring

- **sRPE method (Foster, 2001)**: **Session RPE × Session Duration (min) = Training Load (AU)**. Weekly load = Σ daily loads. **Monotony = mean / SD of weekly loads**; **Strain = total weekly load × monotony**.
- **Impellizzeri, Rampinini, Coutts, Sassi & Marcora (2004) *Med Sci Sports Exerc* 36(6): 1042–1047** validated sRPE against HR and blood lactate in soccer (r = 0.50–0.85).
- **ACWR (Gabbett, 2016; Hulin et al., 2014)**: **ACWR = Acute Load (last 7 d) / Chronic Load (last 28 d, rolling avg or EWMA)**. "Sweet spot" 0.8–1.3; "danger zone" >1.5.
- **Impellizzeri, Woodcock, Coutts, Fanchini, McCall & Vigotsky (2021) "What Role Do Chronic Workloads Play in the Acute to Chronic Workload Ratio? Time to Dismiss ACWR and Its Underlying Theory", *Sports Medicine* 51: 581–592** — demonstrated that **substituting the actual chronic load with randomised values yields equivalent injury associations** (OR 1.95 vs OR 1.16–2.07), exposing ACWR as statistical artefact. Also Impellizzeri, Tenan, Kempton, Novak & Coutts (2020) on conceptual pitfalls.

### 4.8 Injury Prevention

- **FIFA 11+ (Bizzini, Junge & Dvorak, 2013; Soligard et al. 2008 *BMJ*)**: 20-min neuromuscular warm-up. Meta-analyses report **~30–40% reduction in overall lower-extremity injuries (IRR 0.612, 95% CI 0.475–0.788, p=0.001)** (Al Attar et al., 2015) and **~66% reduction in hamstring injury per 1000 h exposure (IRR 0.34, 95% CI 0.21–0.55, p<0.0001)** (2022 systematic review in *J Sci Med Sport*).
- **Nordic Hamstring Exercise**: Petersen, Thorborg, Nielsen, Budtz-Jørgensen & Hölmich (2011 *AJSM*) and the **van Dyk, Behan & Whiteley (2019) "Including the Nordic hamstring exercise in injury prevention programmes halves the rate of hamstring injuries: A systematic review and meta-analysis of 8459 athletes", *BJSM* 53(21): 1362–1370**.

### 4.9 Heart-Rate and GPS Zones

Five-zone HR model (Helgerud et al., 2001 *MSSE*): Z1 <70%, Z2 70–80, Z3 80–87, Z4 87–93, Z5 >93% HRmax. GPS velocity zones (Bradley/Dwyer/Gabbett conventions): walking <7.2; jogging 7.2–14.4; running 14.4–19.8; **HIR 19.8–25.2; sprint >25.2 km/h** (>7 m·s⁻¹).

## 5. COGNITIVE & PERCEPTUAL ASPECTS

### 5.1 Visual Scanning — Jordet's Programme

**Geir Jordet (Norwegian School of Sport Sciences)** initiated the modern scanning literature with Jordet (2005) on imagery, and **Jordet, Bloomfield & Heijmerikx (2013)** "The hidden foundation of field vision in English Premier League soccer players" (presented MIT Sloan; influential despite limited journal indexing) — showed midfielders scanned **~0.5–0.6 scans/s in the 10 s before reception** and that scan rate correlated with completion-probability.

**Jordet, Aksum, Pedersen, Walvekar, Trivedi, McCall, Ivarsson & Priestley (2020) "Scanning, Contextual Factors, and Association With Performance in English Premier League Footballers", *Frontiers in Psychology* 11: 553813** — across a full EPL season, Bayesian hierarchical modelling on 27 players showed scanning frequency had a **small but positive effect on pass completion**, with opponent pressure and pitch position as moderating constraints.

**McGuckian, Cole, Chalkley, Jordet & Pepping (2018, 2019, 2020) — *Frontiers in Psychology* and *J Sports Sci*** documented that higher scanning predicts faster passing response and more forward passes. **Aksum, Pokolm, Bjørndal, Rein, Memmert & Jordet (2021) "Scanning activity in elite youth football players", *J Sports Sci* 39(21)** — U19 > U17 scan rate; central midfielders and CBs scan most; positive relationship between scan frequency and pass success at youth level.

### 5.2 Executive Function

**Vestberg, Gustafson, Maurex, Ingvar & Petrovic (2012) "Executive Functions Predict the Success of Top-Soccer Players", *PLOS ONE* 7(4): e34731** — Swedish national-league players (high division) scored significantly above lower-division on **Design Fluency**, **Color-Word Interference (Stroop variant)** and **Trail-Making** D-KEFS subtests, both groups above population norms. Two-year follow-up: **executive scores predicted future goals + assists**.

**Vestberg, Reinebo, Maurex, Ingvar & Petrovic (2017)** replicated in elite male U17 vs amateur. **Verburgh, Scherder, van Lange & Oosterlaan (2014) *PLOS ONE*** on motor inhibition in Dutch youth elite. Yongtawee et al. (2022) on team-sport vs non-strategic-sport EF comparisons. The recent **Frontiers in Psychology (2025)** scoping review codifies cognitive flexibility, working memory, inhibitory control and perceptual anticipation as four core EFs for elite football.

### 5.3 Anticipation and Pattern Recognition

Williams & Davids (1998) *Research Quarterly for Exercise and Sport*; **Williams & Ward (2007)** chapter in Williams & Hodges, *Skill Acquisition in Sport*. **Abernethy & Russell (1987); Savelsbergh, Williams, Van der Kamp & Ward (2002)** — kinematic cue extraction.

### 5.4 Deliberate Practice — Critiques

**Ericsson, Krampe & Tesch-Römer (1993)** — 10,000-hour heuristic. **Ford, Ward, Hodges & Williams (2009) "The role of deliberate practice and play in career progression in sport", *High Ability Studies*** — football professionals accumulated *less* structured practice than musicians; *play* during childhood was a stronger discriminator. **Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald (2014)** meta-analysis (*Psychological Science*): deliberate practice explains ~18% of variance in sport, not 80%.

### 5.5 Relative Age Effect (RAE)

**Helsen, van Winckel & Williams (2005) "The relative age effect in youth soccer across Europe", *J Sports Sci*** — Q1 birthdates (Jan–Mar) over-represented at ~2:1 vs Q4. **Cobley, Baker, Wattie & McKenna (2009) "Annual age-grouping and athlete development: a meta-analytical review of relative age effects in sport", *Sports Medicine* 39(3): 235–256** — pooled OR ≈ 1.4 across team sports, strongest in U15–U18 elite categories.

### 5.6 Stress, Anxiety and Penalty Performance

**Jordet (2009) *J Sports Sci*** — international penalty shootout data: high-pressure penalties show shorter preparation time and worse outcomes; gaze behaviour deteriorates. **Furley & Memmert (2015)** on attentional foci under pressure.

## 6. COLLECTIVE TACTICAL BEHAVIOUR — DYNAMICAL SYSTEMS

### 6.1 Collective Variables

- **Team centroid** (geometric mean of outfield-player positions) — **Frencken, Lemmink, Delleman & Visscher (2011) "Oscillations of centroid position and surface area of soccer teams in small-sided games", *European Journal of Sport Science* 11(4): 215–223**.
- **Inter-team centroid distance** (Frencken, de Poel, Visscher & Lemmink, 2012) — proxy for pressure.
- **Stretch Index** — mean distance of each player to the centroid (**Bourbousson, Sève & McGarry, 2010 *J Sports Sci*** on basketball; extended to football by Yue, Broich, Seifriz & Mester, 2008; Moura et al., 2012).
- **Length, Width, L/W ratio, Surface Area, Effective Playing Space** — **Folgado, Lemmink, Frencken & Sampaio (2014) "Length, width and centroid distance as measures of teams tactical performance in youth football", *European Journal of Sport Science* 14(S1): S487–S492**.
- **Voronoi diagrams** — Fonseca, Milho, Travassos & Araújo (2012) *Human Movement Science*.

### 6.2 Synchronisation

**Duarte, Araújo, Correia, Davids, Marques & Richardson (2013) "Competing together: Assessing the dynamics of team–team and player–team synchrony in professional association football", *Human Movement Science* 32: 555–566** — cross-correlation between longitudinal displacements quantifies inter-team coupling. Sample entropy and cross-sample entropy (Folgado et al., 2014b) measure regularity. Successful defensive sets show **lower UCM (Uncontrolled Manifold) values** — i.e. less individual adjustment required to stabilise the stretch index — indicating efficient synergy.

### 6.3 Network Analysis

**Buchheit (2016)**; **Buldú, Busquets, Echegoyen & Seirul·lo (2019) "Defining a historic football team: Using Network Science to analyze Guardiola's FC Barcelona", *Scientific Reports* 9: 13602** — eigenvector centrality, clustering coefficient and the *small-world index* characterised tiki-taka. **Clemente, Martins, Kalamaras, Wong & Mendes (2015) *Hum Mov Sci*** on passing-network centrality across Champions League knockouts.

## 7. ATTACKING ORGANISATION — ACADEMIC

### 7.1 Possession vs Direct Play — The Reep Debate

**Reep & Benjamin (1968) *J Royal Statistical Society***: 80% of goals from ≤3-pass sequences. **Hughes & Franks (2005) "Analysis of passing sequences, shots and goals in soccer", *J Sports Sci* 23(5): 509–514** dismantled the "Reep fallacy" of analysing successful outcomes only: when normalised per 1,000 possessions, **longer sequences yielded more goals for successful teams**; *strike ratio* (goals/shots) favours direct play, but *goals/possession* favours possession for successful sides.

### 7.2 Counter-Attack Effectiveness

**Tenga, Holme, Ronglan & Bahr (2010) "Effect of playing tactics on goal scoring in Norwegian professional soccer", *J Sports Sci* 28(3): 237–244** — counter-attacks vs elaborate attacks compared against organised vs disorganised defences; **counter-attacks yielded significantly higher score-box probability vs imbalanced defences**.

### 7.3 Crossing

**Pulis, T., Bate & Hughes (1996)**; **Casal, Andújar, Losada, Ardá & Maneiro (2016) *J Hum Kinetics*** — crosses converted at ~2% efficiency; cut-back / low-driven crosses outperform high crosses. Vecino & Casáis (Spanish Primera) corroborate.

### 7.4 Set Pieces

**Casal, Maneiro, Ardá, Losada & Rial (2015) "Analysis of corner kick success in elite football", *Int J Performance Analysis in Sport* 15(2)** — short corners and near-post deliveries marginally more efficient. **Power et al. (2018) StatsBomb** on xG-from-set-pieces. Set pieces produce ~25–33% of goals in major leagues.

### 7.5 Width and Depth

Castellano, Casamichana & Lago (2012) on EPS; Fradua et al. (2013) operationalised offensive width.

## 8. DEFENSIVE ORGANISATION — ACADEMIC

### 8.1 Block Depth and Compactness

**Castellano, Álvarez-Pastor & Bradley (2014) *Sports Medicine*** — defensive line height correlates with HIR demand on CBs.

### 8.2 Compactness

Inter-line distance (defensive-line to attacking-line) ≤25 m characterises top teams in possession; expansion to 35–45 m on counter. Vertical compactness (FIFA Technical Report 2018, 2022) is decisive.

### 8.3 Marking Systems

**Sampaio & Maçãs (2012); Almeida, Ferreira & Volossovitch (2014)** on man-marking vs zonal recovery patterns. Hybrid systems dominate elite.

**Vogelbein, Nopp & Hökelmann (2014, *J Sports Sci*)** — reaction time to possession loss differentiated table position; **Ruiz-Ruiz, Fradua, Fernández-García & Zubillaga (2013)** on transitional speed metrics.

## 10. TRAINING FOCUS DESIGN — IMPLICATIONS FOR ELIFOOT

### 10.1 In-Game Training Modules — A Morphocycle Engine

Build a weekly engine where the player chooses a **Game Model** (e.g., Posicional, Direto, Reativo-Counter, Pressing Alto) which generates a *Principles tree*. Each MD-day unlocks specific drill categories:

- **MD+1 / +2 (Recovery)**: 5v5+GK in 30×25m, ball-circulation only, technical refinement; load <300 AU (sRPE).
- **MD–4 "Tension" (Wed)**: 3v3 / 4v4 in 25×20 m, max 4 min × 4 sets with 4 min recovery; targets eccentric force, intra-sector principles. Load 500–700 AU.
- **MD–3 "Duration" (Thu)**: 8v8+GK on 65×45m, 4 × 12 min with positional sub-principles; the heaviest load 700–900 AU; HIR ~85–110% of match.
- **MD–2 "Velocity" (Fri)**: 5v5 in 40×30m with full recovery; finishing patterns; speed and set-pieces. Load 350–450 AU.
- **MD–1 (Sat)**: Tactical walkthrough; 200 AU.

### 10.2 Training-Load Math in Code

```
session_load = RPE_0_10 * duration_min
weekly_load = sum(session_loads_last_7d)
monotony = weekly_load_mean / weekly_load_sd
strain = weekly_load * monotony
ACWR = acute_7d / chronic_28d   # report but warn user (Impellizzeri 2021)
```

Player **fatigue state** is a hidden variable updated daily; injury probability rises with strain spikes, but ELIFOOT should *narratively* surface Impellizzeri's critique through a sports-science staff member to reflect current science.

### 10.3 Player Development (LTAD Critiques)

Balyi's LTAD (1990s) prescribes *windows of trainability* but has been **critiqued by Ford et al. (2011 *J Sports Sci*) and Lloyd & Oliver (2012 *Strength Cond J*) → "Youth Physical Development Model"** as evidence-light. ELIFOOT can model **bio-banding** (Cumming, Lloyd, Oliver, Eisenmann & Malina, 2017 *Strength Cond J*) as a youth-academy mechanic where players group by **% predicted adult height (Khamis-Roche)** rather than chronological age — directly addresses the relative-age-effect bias documented by Helsen and Cobley.

### 10.4 Specific vs General Training

The morphocycle embodies a **>80% specific** training distribution. ELIFOOT can quantify this by tagging every drill with a *specificity score* (0–1) and a *Game Model alignment* score. Higher coupling → faster acquisition of Principles, lower transfer-loss.

### 10.5 Tactical Training as Cognitive Load

Drawing on Sampaio, Lago-Peñas & colleagues (numerous *J Sports Sci* papers) and Práxedes, Del Villar, Pizarro & Moreno (2018), include drill parameters: number of decisions/min, defensive pressure level, pitch zone, numerical advantage/disadvantage. Each modulates *cognitive load*, which an attribute like **"Game Intelligence"** can grow when stressed appropriately.

### 10.6 Synthesis for Story Mode

The narrative could place the player-manager in the lineage of Frade's FADEUP → Mourinho's Porto → modern ecological pluralism. Story arcs can pit *Tactical Periodization purists* against *Differential Learning experimenters* and *Pep-style positional* coaches, each with measurable in-game consequences on player attributes, injury rates, and team synergy variables (stretch index, centroid coupling). Geir Jordet's scanning research becomes an unlockable training module ("Scanning Drills") that improves *Visão de Jogo*. Vestberg's executive-function testing becomes a scouting minigame. Bauer & Anzer's counterpressing model becomes the algorithm behind the "Gegenpressing" tactical instruction.
